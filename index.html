Why songs get stuck in your head — and how to get rid of them

What is an earworm?

  • The science name: Involuntary Musical Imagery (INMI)
  • A short loop — usually a 15–30 second chorus
  • ~90% of people get one at least once a week
  • You didn't choose it — your brain just hits "play"

Chau (~50s) — read aloud:

"So first — what actually is an earworm? 'Earworm' is just the nickname. The real scientific term is Involuntary Musical Imagery, or INMI for short.

And the key word there is involuntary. You don't decide to play the song — your brain just hits the play button on its own.

Here's an interesting detail: it's almost never the whole song. It's a tiny chunk — usually just 15 to 30 seconds, and almost always the catchy chorus. That little loop, on repeat.

And it is incredibly common. Surveys show that about 90% of people get an earworm at least once a week. So if it happens to you — you are completely normal. It's basically everyone."

Why does it happen? 🧠

Your brain hates unfinished things.

  • It's called the Zeigarnik effect
  • We remember unfinished tasks better than finished ones
  • An unfinished song = an open loop → brain keeps replaying it to "close" it

Chau (~60s) — read aloud:

"Okay, so why does this happen? And this is my favorite part.

It comes down to a psychology principle called the Zeigarnik effect. The idea is simple: our brains hold on to unfinished things way more than finished ones.

Think about your to-do list. The tasks you've already done? You forget them instantly. But that one thing you haven't done yet — it keeps nagging at the back of your mind. That's the Zeigarnik effect.

Now here's the connection: a song stuck in your head is basically an open loop. It's an unfinished task. So your brain keeps hitting replay, over and over, trying to 'complete' it and close the loop.

Hold on to that idea — 'closing the loop' — because it's going to be the secret to the cure that Tri shows you in a few minutes."

What sets it off?

  • 🎧 You heard the song recently (or a lot)
  • 😬 You're stressed or tired
  • 😶‍🌫️ Your mind is idle — boring or low-focus tasks

An empty mind is the perfect stage for a song.

Chau (~45s) — read aloud, then hand off:

"So what actually sets an earworm off? There are three big triggers.

One: you heard the song recently, or you heard it a lot. Makes sense — it's fresh in there.

Two: you're stressed or tired. When you're run down, earworms get stronger and stick around longer.

And three — this is the big one — when your mind is idle. Doing the dishes, walking, taking a shower, doing some boring repetitive task. When your brain has nothing to do, it fills the silence with music. An empty mind is the perfect stage for a song.

How to actually kill it ✅

  • ▶️ Finish the song — all the way through. Close the loop.
  • 🍬 Chew gum — genuinely, there's a study. It works.
  • 🧩 Give your brain something slightly hard to do — not too hard, not too easy
  • 🎶 Nuke it with a "cure song" — something boring enough to overwrite it

Speaker B (~70s) — the payoff:

"Okay so Chau mentioned the open loop thing — your brain replaying a song to 'finish' it. So the most obvious fix is: just finish it. Listen to the whole song. Your brain goes 'okay, done, we can move on.' Loop closed."

"But my personal favorite — chew gum. This sounds like something I made up, but there's an actual study from the University of Reading. Chewing physically interferes with the same motor system your brain uses to 'sing' internally. Like, you're literally jamming the signal."

"Three: give your brain something mildly engaging to do. A puzzle, a conversation, a mildly annoying email. The key word is *mildly* — if it's too boring the earworm fills the silence again, too hard and you rage-quit. Sweet spot."

"And four — the nuclear option — play something so bland your brain just gives up. Happy Birthday works. So does like, the hold music from your insurance company."

Things nobody asked for but here we are 🎁

  • 🎸 The more music you listen to, the more earworms you get. Congratulations.
  • 😴 They hit harder when you're stressed or sleep-deprived
  • ♾️ A chronic version exists — but for most people it's just annoying, not clinical

Speaker B (~40s):

"Few bonus facts real quick. If you're a musician, or you just listen to a lot of music — bad news — you get earworms more often than everyone else. You've loaded more songs into your brain's shuffle queue. That's the tax you pay for having good taste."

"They also get worse when you're stressed or tired. Which is fun, because those are exactly the moments when you least want a random song looping in your skull."

"And there is actually a chronic version — like people who can't make it stop for days. That's tied to certain conditions and is a whole other thing. But for basically everyone in this room? It's just annoying. You're fine. Probably."
